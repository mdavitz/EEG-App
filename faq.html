Frequently Asked Questions(FAQ)

General EEG Questions

What is an EEG and why is it important?

An EEG (electroencephalogram) is a noninvasive test that records the brain’s electrical activity through electrodes placed on the scalp. It is essential for diagnosing neurological conditions such as epilepsy.

How is an EEG performed?

During an EEG, electrodes are attached to the scalp using conductive paste. The recording is done while the patient is at rest and may include periods of eye opening/closing or hyperventilation to provoke abnormalities.

How should I prepare for an EEG?

Patients are generally advised to avoid caffeine and get adequate sleep prior to the test. Specific instructions may vary based on the protocol of the testing facility.

Seizure & Epilepsy FAQs

When should an EEG be obtained after a first seizure?

Most guidelines recommend obtaining an EEG within 24–48 hours after a first unprovoked seizure to maximize the chance of capturing epileptiform discharges.

What is status epilepticus?

Status epilepticus is defined as a prolonged seizure (typically lasting more than 5 minutes) or a series of seizures without full recovery between them, and it is a medical emergency.

How do anti-seizure medications affect EEG results?

Anti-seizure medications can dampen epileptiform discharges and alter background rhythms, which may affect the sensitivity of the EEG in detecting abnormalities.

Technical & Procedural FAQs

What do different EEG montages mean?

Montages refer to the various arrangements of electrodes used during an EEG. They help localize electrical activity and differentiate between focal and generalized abnormalities.

How are artifacts differentiated from true epileptiform activity?

Artifacts (from muscle movement, eye blinks, etc.) tend to be irregular, localized, and inconsistent. True epileptiform discharges usually appear with a consistent morphology and spatial distribution.

What common technical issues can affect an EEG?

Issues such as poor electrode contact, 60 Hz interference, and movement artifacts can degrade EEG quality. Proper electrode application and minimizing patient movement help reduce these issues.

Advanced Topics

How has genetic testing impacted epilepsy diagnosis?

Genetic testing has enabled clinicians to identify specific mutations associated with epilepsy, thereby facilitating more precise diagnoses and targeted therapies.

What are examples of precision therapies in epilepsy?

For example, in patients with SCN1A mutations (as seen in Dravet syndrome), sodium channel blockers may worsen seizures, so alternative therapies are recommended. Similarly, mTOR inhibitors are used in tuberous sclerosis-related epilepsy.

Why is understanding EEG patterns important?

A thorough understanding of EEG patterns aids in accurate seizure classification, helps determine appropriate treatment plans, and provides insights into the underlying neurological condition.

Miscellaneous

How do I interpret artifact versus true seizure activity on an EEG?

Interpreting an EEG requires comparing waveforms across multiple leads. Artifacts are typically isolated and vary from one lead to another, whereas true seizure activity shows a consistent pattern across several channels.

What are the limitations of EEG in diagnosing epilepsy?

EEG has limitations such as the potential for false negatives (especially if the seizure is infrequent) and difficulties in capturing deep brain activity. Clinical correlation is always necessary.
